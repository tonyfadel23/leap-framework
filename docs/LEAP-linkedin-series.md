## Post 1 — The Provocation (Tuesday)

Something I understood too late:
Most product teams don't have a prioritization problem.
They have a learning problem.

I spent years watching teams ship 47 features in a quarter and not be able to tell you which ones moved the metric. The roadmap was full. The backlog was groomed. The velocity was high. And the product was no better.

The problem is not speed of execution. It is absence of signal.

We measure output — features shipped, story points burned, sprints completed. None of these tell you whether you solved a customer problem. None of them tell you whether the thing you built was worth building. They tell you the team was busy. Busy is not the same as right.

I built LEAP — Learn, Explore, Action, Prove — because I needed a system where the unit of progress is not the feature. It is the resolved bet. A bet with a hypothesis, a kill criteria, and evidence that tells you what happened and why.

A resolved bet has three possible outcomes that matter: ship it, change direction, or kill it. All three are progress. Only one results in a feature. The other two save you months.

The teams I have watched struggle the most are not the ones that ship too little. They are the ones that ship too much — without signal. They build, they launch, they move on. Nothing compounds. Nothing informs the next decision. Every quarter is a fresh start with a blank page and a gut feeling.

LEAP changes the question. Not "what should we build next?" but "what is the highest-pain problem worth solving, and what is the smallest bet that gives us signal?"

TAP — Total Addressable Pain — replaces TAM as the scoring lens. Reach times Frequency times Intensity. Pain drives behavior change. Revenue projections don't.

The product roadmap is a graveyard of unvalidated assumptions. LEAP replaces it with a system that makes learning visible, kills cheap, and compounds over time.

If your team shipped 30 features last quarter and you cannot point to 5 resolved bets with evidence — you don't have a product process. You have a feature factory with good intentions.

The question for Monday morning: Can you name the last feature your team killed — and the evidence that led to the kill?

If not, that is your starting point.

## Post 2 — The Diagnosis (Thursday)

The dirty secret about prioritization frameworks:
They all assume you already know the right problem.
That assumption is where the damage happens.

RICE, ICE, MoSCoW, weighted scoring — every one of these starts after the most important decision has already been made. They help you rank solutions. They do not help you find problems worth solving.

This is the Focusing Illusion applied to product management. Daniel Kahneman named it: "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it." The feature your team is debating right now feels like the most important thing in the world. It is not. It is just the thing that is in front of you.

Prioritization frameworks make this worse. They give rigor to a decision that was never validated. You score five features, pick the one with the highest number, and feel disciplined about it. But the five features were already filtered by recency bias, HiPPO opinions, and whatever the loudest customer asked for last week.

The second failure: isolated validation. A team runs a prototype test. Users like it. Ship it. But the test never asked: is this the right problem? Is this the right stage of the customer relationship to invest in?

I watched a team build a beautiful recommendation engine for a segment that could not find their last order. Functional-stage customers. Search was broken. The recommendation engine scored well in isolation. In context, it was building a second floor on a house with no foundation.

This is why LEAP starts with the Customer Relationship Hierarchy: Functional, Trustworthy, Engaging, Valuable, Loved. You do not build personalization when reliability is broken. You do not build delight when customers cannot trust your delivery time. The hierarchy killed that recommendation project in one question: "What relationship stage are we in with this segment?"

The diagnosis is structural, not operational:

1. Prioritization frameworks rank solutions. They do not validate problems.
2. Isolated validation confirms desirability. It does not confirm importance.
3. Without a relationship-stage filter, teams build for aspirational customers while neglecting real ones.

The fix is not a better scoring model. The fix is a system that forces you to answer three questions before you touch a solution: What problem is worth solving? For whom? And why now?

LEAP calls this stage LEARN. Most teams skip it entirely.

What to do Monday morning: Take your top three roadmap items. For each one, write the problem it solves, the customer segment, and the relationship stage. If you cannot — you are prioritizing solutions to unnamed problems.

## Post 3 — The Framework (Tuesday)

LEAP has three stages and one layer.
That distinction matters more than the acronym.

**LEARN. EXPLORE. ACTION.** Sequential in concept, fluid in practice.

**PROVE** is not a stage. It is a continuous evaluation layer that runs across all three. At every point in the loop, PROVE asks: based on what we now know, should we continue, change direction, or stop?

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║  P R O V E  (continuous evaluation layer)        ║
║  Ship · Iterate · Pivot · Kill                   ║
║                                                  ║
║  ┌───────┐     ┌─────────┐     ┌────────┐      ║
║  │ LEARN │────▶│ EXPLORE │────▶│ ACTION │      ║
║  └───┬───┘     └─────────┘     └───┬────┘      ║
║      │                              │            ║
║      └────── Loop (enriched) ───────┘            ║
║                                                  ║
║  Ship ──▶ Exits loop → Scale & Operate           ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

**LEARN** answers: what problem is worth solving, for whom, and why now? Every opportunity is scored using your chosen rationalisation framework — Total Addressable Pain. Reach times Frequency times Intensity. Then filtered by the Customer Relationship Hierarchy: Functional, Trustworthy, Engaging, Valuable, Loved. Maslow for product. Solve the current stage before building for the next.

LEARN produces a strategy document with a metric, a baseline, a target, and a scored opportunity map.

**EXPLORE** generates alternatives, not confirmations. Not one solution — at least three. Each materially different enough to test a different assumption. The engine: Diverge, Stress-Test, Converge. The stress test runs a pre-mortem — classify risks as Tigers (real threats), Paper Tigers (fears that dissolve on inspection), or Elephants (obvious problems nobody names).

EXPLORE produces a product brief, a working prototype, and a pre-mortem.

**ACTION** turns solutions into bets. Every bet has a size that matches your confidence:

- **Scout** — hours to 2 days. Low confidence. One signal.
- **Sprint** — 1-2 weeks. Medium confidence. Evidence suggests this matters.
- **Campaign** — 2-4 weeks. High confidence. Validated in Scout or Sprint.
- **Moonshot** — 4-8 weeks. Strategic commitment.

Every bet defines its kill criteria before execution starts. Hypothesis, measurement, and prove criteria: ship if A, iterate if B, pivot if C, kill if D. Pre-commitment. You decide what the results mean before sunk cost bias arrives.

**PROVE** routes the outcome. Ship exits the loop. Iterate creates a sibling bet. Pivot sends you back to EXPLORE. Kill archives with evidence. The tree remembers everything.

The framework is a loop, not a line. Every pass through the loop adds evidence. Every resolved bet makes the next one smarter.

What to do Monday morning: Pick one item from your roadmap. Map it to LEARN, EXPLORE, or ACTION. If it is in ACTION without having passed through LEARN — you are building on assumptions.

## Post 4 — The Kill (Thursday)

The most valuable outcome of a product bet is not the ship.
It is the kill.

This will feel wrong. Product teams are rewarded for shipping. Promotions, launch posts, case studies — all of it celebrates the thing that went live. Nobody writes a case study about the thing they stopped building.

But the math does not care about your feelings.

A Scout bet takes four hours. If it produces a kill with evidence, you just saved your team 8-12 weeks of building the wrong thing. That is not a failure. That is the highest-ROI outcome in product management.

A kill with evidence does three things no ship can do:

**1. It creates organizational memory.** Six months from now, someone will have the same idea. In a traditional team, they pitch it, it gets debated, it gets prioritized, and the cycle starts again. In LEAP, they open the tree and find: "We tested this. Here is what happened. Here is why we stopped." The kill saves the organization from repeating itself. Ships do not do this.

**2. It compounds learning.** Every kill adds a rationale to the system. The AI reads all previous kills before generating new opportunities. The 10th bet in a workspace is structurally smarter than the first because it stands on nine resolved cycles — including the kills. A ship adds a feature. A kill adds a constraint. Constraints are more valuable than features because they narrow the search space.

**3. It protects focus.** Every feature you ship creates maintenance cost, cognitive load, and opportunity cost. A feature that should have been killed but shipped anyway is not neutral. It is negative. It consumes engineering time, support bandwidth, and product attention for years. The kill protects the team from the slow accumulation of mediocre features that nobody wanted to stop.

LEAP operationalizes this through pre-committed prove criteria. Before the bet starts, you define: ship if A, iterate if B, pivot if C, kill if D. The decision is made before the sunk cost arrives. You are not deciding whether to kill something after three weeks of building it — surrounded by organizational pressure to ship. You decided the criteria on day one.

The quality scoring system reinforces it. Every candidate is scored 0-100. Above 70: **publish** to the tree. 50-69: **watch** — parked for later. Below 50: **park** — logged and archived. The system generates freely and filters ruthlessly.

This is the cultural shift LEAP demands. Celebrate the kill. Document the evidence. Move faster because you stopped sooner.

What to do Monday morning: Find the weakest item on your current roadmap. Write the kill criteria. If you cannot articulate what failure looks like — you have no way to stop.

## Post 5 — The AI Shift (Tuesday)

LEAP is not AI-augmented product management.
It is AI-first product management.
The difference is structural, not cosmetic.

Every product tool in the market is doing the same thing: taking the existing PM workflow and adding AI. Write a PRD faster. Summarize user interviews. Auto-generate acceptance criteria. AI as autocomplete for the same broken process.

This is a horse carriage with a motor bolted on. It is faster. It goes to the same place.

LEAP was designed from scratch assuming AI exists. Not adapted. Designed. Remove the AI and you do not get a simpler framework. You get nothing. Like removing the engine from an automobile — it is not a slower car. It is a sculpture.

Five architectural decisions make this real:

**Skills, not templates.** Traditional frameworks give you a blank PRD to fill in. LEAP gives you AI-powered skills that generate complete artifacts from context. The PM does not write. The PM reviews, critiques, and decides. Less writer. More judge.

**Parallel synthesis, not sequential review.** Three AI agents analyze the same opportunity simultaneously — Strategist (market lens), Researcher (user lens), Analyst (systems lens). A synthesis agent merges their perspectives. The PM gets a multi-perspective analysis in minutes that would take a cross-functional team days. You cannot parallelize humans. You can parallelize agents.

**Quality scoring as a filter.** Every candidate scored 0-100. Publish, watch, or park. No artificial "generate 3-5 ideas" quota. The AI generates all worthy candidates. Quality gates filter automatically. Generation is cheap. Judgment is expensive. Spend your time on judgment.

**Autopilot as the default.** The entire discovery loop runs autonomously — strategy sprint to opportunity discovery to product briefs to prototypes to experiments. The PM reviews at decision points. The AI handles everything between.

**Compound context, not fresh starts.** Every cycle enriches the tree. Every resolved bet adds evidence. Every kill adds a rationale. The AI reads all of it before generating anything new. The 10th bet is smarter than the first because it stands on nine previous cycles.

The PM's role does not shrink. It elevates. The PM sets the goal. Critiques the strategy. Challenges the scoring. Picks the bet size. Defines the kill criteria. Makes the ship/iterate/pivot/kill call. The thinking gets harder, not easier. The decisions get better because the evidence is richer.

Two years ago, a prototype took two weeks. Now it takes two hours. You generate five interactive prototypes in a morning and show them to users by lunch. This workflow is impossible without AI. It was not added to the framework. The framework was built around it.

What to do Monday morning: List every artifact your team produces manually. For each one, ask: could this be generated from context and reviewed instead of written from scratch? That list is your automation backlog.

## Post 6 — The Compound Effect (Thursday)

The team that runs LEAP for three quarters will structurally outperform
the team that ships twice as many features.
This is not motivation. It is math.

Here is what happens quarter by quarter:

**Quarter 1 — Learning the system.** The team runs mostly Scout bets. Four hours, one signal. They resolve 5 bets. Some ship. Some kill. Every outcome adds evidence to the tree. The team is uncomfortable because they are shipping less and learning more. That discomfort is the system working.

**Quarter 2 — The evidence starts compounding.** Every new bet starts with Q1's evidence already loaded. The AI reads all previous resolved bets before generating anything new. Kill decisions that took weeks now take days because the context is richer. The team resolves 12 bets. Not because they work harder. Because each bet starts smarter and resolves faster.

**Quarter 3 — The flywheel is spinning.** New bets inherit 50+ previous evidence artifacts. The AI's opportunity scoring is sharper because it knows what has been tested, what failed, and why. The team resolves 18+ bets. Kill confidence is high. Ship confidence is higher. The tree is a living system of organizational memory that no competitor can replicate by hiring your PM.

The causal chain: more resolved bets produce more evidence, which produces better-informed future bets, which resolve faster, which produces more resolved bets. This is a compounding data flywheel. It does not exist in a world where every PRD starts from a blank page.

The metric that matters: **bets resolved per quarter.** Not features shipped. Not story points burned. Not roadmap completion percentage. Bets resolved with evidence.

A team resolving 15 bets with evidence will outperform a team shipping 50 features without signal. Every single time. Because the first team knows what works, what does not, and why. The second team knows they were busy.

This is also why LEAP creates a structural moat. The compound context is specific to your product, your customers, your market. It cannot be copied. A competitor can copy your feature. They cannot copy 50 resolved bets of evidence about why that feature exists and what alternatives were killed to get there.

Traditional PM tools do not compound. Each quarter is a fresh start. Each PRD begins from scratch. Each research project reinvents the wheel. LEAP was designed so that the system gets better at your specific product with every cycle. Organizational learning is not a side effect. It is the product.

What to do Monday morning: Count how many bets your team resolved last quarter with documented evidence. If the number is less than 5, you are operating without compounding. Start with one Scout bet this week. Resolve it by Friday. Begin the flywheel.

## Post 7 — The Monday Morning Playbook (Tuesday)

You have read six posts about LEAP.
Here is how to start running it in 10 days.
No tool required. No permission needed. Just discipline.

**Day 1 (Monday) — Set the goal.**
Pick one goal. Not three. One. Write it as a metric with a baseline and a target. "Increase weekly active buyers from 12K to 15K by end of Q3." If your goal does not have a number, it is a wish.

**Day 2 (Tuesday) — Map the pain.**
List every customer problem related to that goal. Score each using your chosen rationalisation framework — TAP (reach × frequency × intensity), ICE, RICE, or whatever fits your context. The point is forced ranking, not the specific formula. Pick the highest-scored opportunity. Write it in one sentence.

**Day 3 (Wednesday) — Check the relationship stage.**
Apply the Customer Relationship Hierarchy: Functional, Trustworthy, Engaging, Valuable, Loved. What stage is your customer segment in? If the answer is Functional or Trustworthy, kill any solution that targets Engaging or above. Solve the current stage first.

**Day 4 (Thursday) — Generate three solutions.**
Not one. Three. Each must test a different assumption. Spend 30 minutes on a pre-mortem for each: "It is six months from now and this failed. What happened?" Classify risks as Tigers (real threats), Paper Tigers (dissolve on inspection), or Elephants (obvious problems nobody names).

**Day 5 (Friday) — Pick the bet and size it.**
Select the solution with the best risk-to-signal ratio. Size it as a Scout — hours to 2 days, low confidence, one signal. Write the bet card:
- Hypothesis: "If we [do X], then [metric Y] will [move by Z]"
- Kill criteria: "We stop if [specific condition]"
- Ship criteria: "We proceed if [specific condition]"

**Days 6-8 (Monday-Wednesday) — Execute the Scout.**
Build the minimum thing that produces signal. Not the minimum viable product. The minimum viable experiment. A prototype. A concierge test. A landing page. Whatever gives you data in 48 hours.

**Day 9 (Thursday) — Read the evidence.**
Compare results to your pre-committed criteria. Ship, iterate, pivot, or kill. Write down what you learned and why. This is the evidence artifact. It is more valuable than the experiment itself.

**Day 10 (Friday) — Route the decision.**
- **Ship** — write the launch plan. Exit the loop.
- **Iterate** — adjust the hypothesis. Run another Scout.
- **Pivot** — same problem, different solution. Go back to Day 4.
- **Kill** — archive with evidence. Celebrate it. You just saved your team months.

Then start Day 1 again with the next goal or the next opportunity. Every cycle adds evidence. Every cycle makes the next one smarter. By the end of the quarter, you are not running a process. You are running a compounding system.

The system sells itself after the first kill. That is when the team realizes they saved three months of building the wrong thing. And they have the evidence to prove it.

Build less. Learn more. Let the evidence decide.
