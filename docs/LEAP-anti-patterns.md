# The 7 Product Diseases LEAP Was Built to Kill

> Every framework is a response to a failure pattern. LEAP was not designed in a vacuum — it was designed against specific, named dysfunctions that destroy product teams. These are the diseases. LEAP is the immune system.

---

## 1. The Focusing Illusion — Validating Problems in Isolation

**Symptom:** A team spends six weeks researching one opportunity. The research is thorough. The slides are beautiful. Nobody asked whether this problem ranks above the other fifteen problems the customer has.

**Root cause:** Teams conflate depth with rigor. They go deep on the first interesting problem they find, without ever ranking it against alternatives. The TAM spreadsheet says the market is big. Nobody measured whether the pain is real.

**How LEAP kills it:** TAP scoring — Total Addressable Pain. Reach × Frequency × Intensity. Every opportunity is ranked before any opportunity is researched. The Customer Relationship Hierarchy adds a second filter: solve the current stage before building for the next. You don't get to validate a problem in isolation because the system forces comparison before commitment.

---

## 2. The First-Idea Trap — Committing to One Solution Without Alternatives

**Symptom:** The team has one solution. They refine it for weeks. Nobody generates alternatives because the first idea "felt right." The PM writes a PRD. Engineering estimates. Design mocks up. Nobody asks: "What if we're solving this the wrong way?"

**Root cause:** Generating alternatives feels wasteful when you already have an answer. It is not. One solution means zero comparison. Zero comparison means zero signal about which approach carries the least risk.

**How LEAP kills it:** EXPLORE requires divergence — at least three materially different solutions before convergence. The stress-test pre-mortem classifies risks as Tigers (real threats), Paper Tigers (fears that dissolve on inspection), and Elephants (obvious problems nobody names). AI generates interactive prototypes for each approach in hours. You show five prototypes to users by lunch. The cost of alternatives dropped to near-zero. The excuse for skipping them disappeared with it.

---

## 3. Sunk Cost Escalation — Building Past Kill Criteria

**Symptom:** The experiment data came back inconclusive. The team asks for "two more weeks." Then two more. The investment grows. The evidence does not. Nobody will say: "This is not working."

**Root cause:** Kill criteria were never defined before execution started. Or they were defined and then renegotiated when the data arrived. Sunk cost bias is not a character flaw — it is the predictable result of making decisions about results in the moment you see the results.

**How LEAP kills it:** The bet card defines Prove criteria before execution begins. Ship if A. Iterate if B. Pivot if C. Kill if D. This is pre-commitment. The decision is made when the team is rational — not when they are staring at six weeks of sunk work. PROVE runs as a continuous evaluation layer, not a retrospective. The question "should we continue?" is structural, not optional.

---

## 4. The Zombie Project — Nobody Will Kill It

**Symptom:** Everyone knows the project is not going to deliver meaningful impact. Nobody says it. The project continues because a senior leader proposed it, or because the team was already allocated, or because killing it feels like admitting failure. It consumes resources and produces nothing.

**Root cause:** Organizations lack a mechanism for dignified kills. Killing a project feels personal. The learning is lost. The team feels punished.

**How LEAP kills it:** The kill is the most valuable outcome in LEAP. A kill with evidence saves months of building the wrong thing. Every killed branch is archived with full artifacts — prototypes, data, rationale. Six months later, someone proposes the same idea. They open the tree and find: "We tested this. Here's what happened. Here's why we stopped." The kill becomes organizational memory, not organizational shame. Bet sizing adds a structural safeguard: low confidence gets a Scout (hours, not weeks). You cannot zombie a Scout. The investment is too small to sustain denial.

---

## 5. The Feature Factory — Shipping Without Learning

**Symptom:** The team ships 50 features in a quarter. Nobody can tell you which ones moved a metric. The roadmap is a list of things customers asked for. The backlog grows. Impact does not.

**Root cause:** The unit of progress is the feature, not the learning. Shipping is celebrated. Learning is not measured. There is no feedback loop between what shipped and what happened next.

**How LEAP kills it:** The unit of progress in LEAP is the resolved bet. Not the shipped feature. Every bet has a hypothesis, a measurement plan, and Prove criteria defined before execution. Evidence compounds in the tree — every resolved bet enriches every future bet. The metric that matters: bets resolved per quarter. A team resolving 15 bets with evidence will outperform a team shipping 50 features without signal. Every single time.

---

## 6. Relationship Stage Mismatch — Building Delight When Basics Are Broken

**Symptom:** The team builds a recommendation engine while customers cannot find their last order. A loyalty program launches while delivery reliability is 70%. The roadmap is full of ambitious features solving problems the customer does not have yet — because the team skipped the problems the customer has now.

**Root cause:** Teams optimize for what is exciting, not what is necessary. Delight is more fun to build than reliability. Nobody gets promoted for fixing search. Everybody gets promoted for launching personalization.

**How LEAP kills it:** The Customer Relationship Hierarchy — Functional → Trustworthy → Engaging → Valuable → Loved. This is Maslow for product. You do not build personalization when search is broken. You do not build delight when customers cannot trust your delivery time. The hierarchy is embedded in strategy.md and filters every opportunity through the current relationship stage. A team once pitched a beautiful recommendation engine. One question killed the project: "What relationship stage are we in with this segment?" Functional. The hierarchy did its job.

---

## 7. Institutional Amnesia — Losing Learning When People Leave

**Symptom:** A PM leaves. Their research leaves with them. A new PM joins, spends three months rediscovering what the previous team already knew. The same failed ideas get proposed again because nobody remembers they were tested.

**Root cause:** Knowledge lives in people's heads, not in systems. Confluence pages decay. Nobody reads the retrospective from two quarters ago. Tribal knowledge is the default, and it evaporates on attrition.

**How LEAP kills it:** The tree is the system of record. Every cycle — every opportunity, every solution, every bet, every kill — is a node with full context. The AI reads the entire tree before generating anything new. The 10th bet in a workspace is smarter than the first because it stands on nine previous cycles. Compound context replaces fresh starts. When a PM leaves, the tree stays. When a new PM joins, the AI synthesizes the complete history in seconds. Institutional memory is structural, not personal.

---

## The One-Line Diagnostic

Ask your team: **"What hypothesis does your current work test?"**

If the answer is silence, you have at least three of these diseases. LEAP is the treatment.
